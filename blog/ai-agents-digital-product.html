How I Used AI Agents to Build and Launch a Digital Product in a Week

Fernando Fernandez 7 min read 1,310 words
An honest look at what gets automated, what doesn't, and what the actual numbers are.

I launched a digital product last week.

The research was done by an agent. The SEO posts were written by an agent. The HTML landing page was built by an agent. The Gumroad listing copy, the email sequence, the social launch posts: all agents.

I wrote the actual guide. About 3,000 words, over a few evenings. That part can't be automated, and I'll explain why in a minute.

But the infrastructure around it (the stuff that usually takes weeks and either kills your momentum or just never gets done) was mostly automated. And the total compute cost to generate all of it was around $40.

This isn't a "make money while you sleep" post. Those are usually lying to you. This is what it actually looked like.

What the agents did

I'm running a system called fernandOS Digital. It's a set of Claude-based agents with specific roles: a researcher, a writer, a builder. They run on cron jobs and write files to a shared workspace. When one finishes, the next one picks up.

For this launch, they produced:

  • A complete competitive research report with pricing analysis and gap analysis
  • A full SEO content strategy with 11 blog post outlines and target keywords
  • All 11 blog posts, written to a style guide calibrated to my voice
  • The landing page HTML (dark/amber design, pricing tiers, FAQ, the whole thing)
  • Gumroad listing copy
  • A 4-email welcome sequence for new subscribers
  • Social launch posts for Reddit, X, and Hacker News
  • A bundle of actual config files and templates that ship with the product
  • A 10-step launch playbook

That list sounds insane. I know. But read the next part before you get too excited.

What the agents couldn't do

The product I'm selling is a guide about how I built this system: the AI agent setup I use, costs, and decisions I made.

No agent can write that.

It can write around it. It can scaffold the structure, write the technical explainers, set up the chapter flow. But the parts that make a guide worth buying are the personal stories. The specific failure I hit three times before I figured out the fix. The month where I spent $90 on Claude API before I implemented model routing and got it down to $3/day. The one cron job that I killed because it was just producing garbage every night.

Those details only exist in my head. An agent writing my experience is just hallucinating. And readers can tell.

So here's the actual split for this launch:

Agent did it (~70% of content volume): Research, SEO posts, landing page HTML, listing copy, email sequence, social posts, bundle infrastructure, launch playbook.

I had to do it (~30% of content volume): The guide itself (3,000 words of my actual experience), filling in specific numbers and anecdotes throughout the agent-written chapters, reviewing everything for accuracy and voice.

Total my time: maybe 12–15 hours over a week. Not counting thinking time. That's it.

Without agents, this same launch would have taken me a month (if I did it at all). Most side projects die because the infrastructure work isn't interesting and there's no one to make it go away.

The specific costs

The agents ran on Claude API. Here's what the compute actually cost:

  • Research + competitive analysis: ~$3
  • 11 SEO blog posts: ~$12
  • Landing page HTML: ~$4
  • Gumroad copy + email sequence: ~$2
  • Social posts: ~$1
  • Bundle infrastructure (code files, templates): ~$6
  • Launch playbook + supporting docs: ~$2
  • Misc iteration and revisions: ~$10

Total: roughly $40.

The guide platform (Gumroad) is free until you sell something. Domain is about $12/year. Hosting is free on Vercel. Email marketing is free on Buttondown up to 100 subscribers.

So the all-in cost before I make a single sale is around $52.

The product is $29. Two sales and I'm profitable on compute costs. That's a different math than anything else I've built.

What kind of product works for this

Not everything is a good candidate. This approach works best when:

You have specific knowledge that others don't. The guide I wrote is useful because I've actually built and run a production AI agent system. I know what breaks. I know what the costs really are. I can tell you things that aren't in any tutorial because I learned them by doing it wrong first.

If you don't have that (if you'd just be summarizing documentation or regurgitating YouTube tutorials), agents aren't going to save you. You'll just produce a polished version of generic.

The product is digital and downloadable. Agents can write guides, produce templates, generate code bundles, design HTML files. They can't ship physical goods. They can't provide services. The economics work when the product costs nothing to duplicate.

You can articulate what you know. The agents need direction. I had to give them context about my actual setup, my actual costs, my actual workflow. If you can't describe what you know well enough to brief an agent, you're not ready to productize it yet.

The piece that's still hard

Distribution.

Agents can write the Reddit posts. They can't post them. They can draft the X thread. They can't build the audience that makes it land.

The content marketing strategy (11 SEO posts targeting long-tail keywords, plus social distribution) is a bet. It's a bet that enough people search for "production AI agent cost" or "how to run Claude on cron jobs" that some of them will find the free guide, get value from it, and buy the $29 bundle.

That bet takes time to pay off. SEO takes months. Reddit posts are either going to work or they won't, and you can't really predict it.

So the launch isn't done. The infrastructure is ready. The products exist. The content is written. Now I wait to see if anyone finds it.

That's the honest version of "passive income." The income isn't there until the distribution is there. And distribution is the part that takes the longest and has the most uncertainty.

How to actually start

If this sounds like something you want to try, here's what I'd suggest:

First, figure out what you know that other people want to know. Not what you think is interesting, but what people actually search for, ask about in forums, pay for in courses and books. If you've solved a specific problem that others are trying to solve, that's the product.

Second, set up the agent infrastructure before you need it. The guide I'm selling covers this in detail, but the short version: you need an agent with memory persistence, a way to run jobs on a schedule, and some system for passing work between agents. That's not complicated, but it takes a few weekends to get right.

Third, write the thing yourself first. Brief the agents heavily, let them produce scaffolding and supporting content, but don't outsource the actual knowledge transfer. That's the part that makes it worth buying.
